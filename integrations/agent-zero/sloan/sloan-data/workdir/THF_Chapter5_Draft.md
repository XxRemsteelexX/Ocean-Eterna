# Chapter 5: The Celestial Record
## Orbital Mechanics, Missing Planets, and Lunar Anomalies

When we look out into the solar system, we assume stability. We see planets operating on predictable clockwork ellipses and assume it has been this way for billions of years. But the celestial record is written in scars. The data tells a story of extreme violence, catastrophic reorganization, and anomalous orbital mechanics.

Take the asteroid belt sitting at 2.7 AU between Mars and Jupiter. Its total mass is absurdly low—just 4% of the Moon—yet it contains highly differentiated geochemical families (C-type, S-type, M-type). Then look at Mars. MAVEN satellite telemetry confirms that the solar wind actively stripped its once-thick atmosphere following the collapse of its magnetosphere. Now gaze at Venus, stubbornly rotating backward (retrograde), requiring a staggering dampening force to reverse its angular momentum. Finally, observe Mercury: an iron-rich core accounting for an impossible 85% of its planetary radius.

### The N-Body Disruption Paradigm
These are not isolated anomalies; they are related data points. Running N-body gravitational simulations, we can recreate the Grand Tack hypothesis or an inner-system disruption model. Mercury's density is entirely consistent with a planetary body that suffered a kinetic impact so massive it physically stripped away its proto-silicate mantle.

And then there is Earth's moon. Isotope ratios between the Earth and Moon are virtually identical. The Giant Impact Hypothesis remains the favored model, but lunar capture mechanics keep producing statistical ghosts when filtered through Monte Carlo orbit insertion paths against the strict Earth-Moon angular momentum budget.

### Testing the Null Hypothesis
Let us map the anomalies against a mathematical threshold.

Null Hypothesis ($H_0$): The isotopic composition and orbital distribution of inner solar system bodies (specifically Mercury's iron-to-silicate ratio and the asteroid belt) fall within the standard variance of a pristine, undisturbed protoplanetary disc formation model without catastrophic disruption.

Applying N-body simulation debris matrices and comparing them against the current observed distributions, we calculate the probability of these anomalies forming without late-stage catastrophic kinetic events. The statistical T-test returns a $p$-value significantly below our 0.05 alpha limit ($p < 0.05$).

We decisively reject the Null Hypothesis. The celestial record demands a catastrophic inner-system reorganization. We live in a neighborhood that reset itself violently—a dynamic that dramatically informs the extinction modeling of our own Paleobiology.
