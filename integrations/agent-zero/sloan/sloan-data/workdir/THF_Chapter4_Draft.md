# Chapter 4: Cities Beneath the Waves
## Sea Level Modeling and Lost Coastal Civilizations

During the Last Glacial Maximum (LGM), roughly 20,000 years ago, the planet was unrecognizable. The massive ice sheets locking up the Earth's water resulted in global sea levels over 120 meters (400 feet) lower than today. 

We know that modern civilizations cluster relentlessly around coastal zones and river deltas—trade, agriculture, and climate stability demand it. In deep antiquity, this rule remained absolute. The Persian Gulf, for example, was completely dry land. It was a massive, fertile river valley fed by the Tigris and Euphrates. This geographical reality neatly overlaps with Sumerian texts detailing pre-flood 'cities of the gods' resting in geographical locations that are currently under the ocean.

### Bathymetry and Settlement Limits
As we reconstruct these coastlines using NOAA paleoclimate bathymetric data, an undeniable data gap emerges. The most temperate, resource-dense land masses during the LGM are now submerged. When the Holocene transgression occurred, the ice melted rapidly. Meltwater pulses periodically drowned millions of square miles of prime continental shelf real estate in geological eyeblinks.

As data scientists, our job is mapping probabilities. The probability of human settlement on the continental shelf before 7000 BCE approaches 1.0. Yet, owing to the crushing physics of water—specifically the 40-meter limit for conventional SCUBA archaeology—these submerged shelves remain more than 95% unexplored.

### Testing the Null Hypothesis
To quantify this, we establish our Null Hypothesis ($H_0$): The probability distribution of discovering ancient macroscopic settlements is uniformly distributed across dry terrestrial land, and coastal submersion has no statistically significant dampening effect on archaeological discovery.

We run a spatial probability model (Monte Carlo method based on settlement proximity to LGM coastlines vs modern land). Calculating the discovery density per square kilometer, the disparity is massive. Running a Pearson correlation yields a $p$-value well under our 0.05 threshold ($p < 0.05$).

We aggressively reject the Null Hypothesis. Discovery rates are not uniform; they are artificially choked point-blank at the shoreline. The dataset we call 'human history' is physically missing its foundational layer, drowned under 400 feet of water.
